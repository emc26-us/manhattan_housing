Speaker Script — 6 Minute Presentation

Slide 1 — Title ~0:40

Good [morning/afternoon]. I want to start with a paradox. Manhattan has some of the highest rents in the world. A median one-bedroom costs about $4,500 a month — which requires an income of $180,000 a year just to be considered affordable under the standard 30% threshold. The median NYC renter earns $56,000. They are spending 96% of their gross income on rent.

So rents are crushing tenants. That part most people know. But here is what is less discussed: landlords are also losing. Manhattan cap rates — the annual yield a landlord earns on the value of their asset — have compressed to about 3%. The historical norm was 5 to 6%. At 3%, the asset is massively overpriced relative to the income it generates. No rational investor buying Manhattan real estate today at market price is earning a normal return.

So we have a situation where rent is simultaneously too high for tenants and too low to justify asset prices. That is the paradox this report resolves. If both parties are losing, the price of the asset itself must be wrong. Today I construct what both the asset price and the rent should be.

Slide 2 — The Paradox ~0:50

Let me put some numbers behind this paradox. On the tenant side: a one-bedroom apartment in Manhattan rents for around $4,500 a month. At the standard HUD affordability threshold of 30% of income, you need to earn $180,000 a year for that rent to be considered affordable. The median NYC renter earns $56,000. They are spending close to their entire income on housing. That is the tenant's problem.

On the landlord side: Manhattan cap rates have compressed to roughly 3%. What does that mean? It means if you buy a Manhattan apartment building at today's market price, your annual rental income is only 3% of what you paid. The long-run historical norm for residential real estate is 5 to 6%. At 3%, the math doesn't work — you're overpaying for the asset relative to what it earns.

So the paradox is real: rents are too high for the people paying them, and too low to justify the prices of the buildings they live in. The only way to resolve this logically is that the asset price itself is inflated — not by genuine housing value, but by something else. Something that drives up purchase prices without proportionally driving up rents.

That something, as I will show, is a bundle of legal instruments — zoning restrictions, permitting barriers, and financial tax treatment — that have inflated the asset value far beyond what legitimate housing economics would produce. Strip those out, and you get a fair asset price of around $770 per square foot and a fair rent of around $3,887 per month for a one-bedroom. Both numbers are lower than today's actuals. Both produce a healthier, more rational market for investors and tenants alike.

Slide 3 — Methodology ~0:45

I'm drawing on three established academic frameworks. The first is Glaeser and Gyourko's zoning tax — their 2003 NBER paper showed that Manhattan prices exceed construction costs by a large margin, and they identified zoning as the primary mechanism. Their methodology is peer-reviewed and widely cited.

The second is Van Nieuwerburgh's asset pricing approach from Columbia Business School — he compares actual prices to what is justified by discounted future rental income. The gap is the financialization premium — the amount people are paying not for shelter but for a financial asset.

The third anchor is Tokyo's Minato-ku district — a city comparable to Manhattan in density and economic productivity, but with more supply-responsive zoning. Tokyo land prices give me a baseline for what genuine locational value looks like without regulatory inflation.

Slide 4 — The Decomposition ~1:15

This is the core of the argument. I'm building Manhattan's price from the ground up, component by component, and stripping out each premium that traces to a specific legal instrument.

Construction costs: I keep $400 per square foot as a legitimate NYC high-rise premium. I eliminate $125 in regulatory inflation — that's prevailing wage mandates above competitive rates, DOB permitting delays, and SEQRA compliance costs.

Land is the biggest story. Manhattan land currently trades at around $500 per square foot of buildable area. I keep $175 — that's the Tokyo comparable, representing genuine island geography. I eliminate $325 that exists purely because the 1961 Zoning Resolution caps FAR below what the market would economically support. That's not scarcity — that's a legal restriction that enriches existing landowners at the expense of everyone else.

Financing costs: I keep $80 — legitimate capital costs over a 2-year competitive development timeline. I eliminate $145 that exists because ULURP stretches that timeline to 3 to 5 years. Every extra year is extra carrying cost, and it flows straight to lenders.

Developer return: I keep a full competitive 5% IRR — about $75 per square foot. That's legitimate. I eliminate the $125 above that, which exists because ULURP barriers to entry protect established developers from competition. That's monopoly rent, not market return.

And finally — the financialization premium. Six hundred dollars per square foot. The entire amount, eliminated. Housing used as a store of wealth for the domestic upper class and global capital has zero legitimacy from a shelter-market perspective. It's enabled by the mortgage interest deduction, 1031 exchange rules, and REIT tax treatment — all federal legal instruments.

The result: a competitive market price of approximately $770 per square foot. Against an actual price of around $1,500.

Slide 5 — Core Statistics ~0:30

Four numbers from this decomposition. First: $770 per square foot — the competitive market baseline. A typical 700 square foot apartment should cost around $539,000, not $1,050,000.

Second: 64% of Manhattan's actual average price is artificial premium with no competitive market justification.

Third: $22 billion per year in aggregate transfers — from buyers and renters to landowners and finance — enforced by law. That's roughly $2,500 per Manhattan resident per year above what they'd pay in a competitive market.

And fourth: translating to rent. A fair one-bedroom in Manhattan should cost around $3,887 per month — covering all legitimate costs at a 6% cap rate, with operating expenses. Against an actual median of about $4,500.

Slide 6 — Class Analysis ~0:30

Who pays? Everyone who rents or buys in Manhattan. But the burden is wildly unequal. A minimum wage worker would spend 162% of their income on actual rent — and 140% even at the fair rent. The median NYC renter would spend 96% of income. A professional earning $120,000 would still spend 45%.

Only at $156,000 annual income does the fair rent become affordable at the standard 30% threshold. Manhattan excludes the bottom 80% of households even without any legal distortion.

Who captures the premium? Manhattan landowners capture about $8 billion annually through the regulatory land premium. Financial institutions capture about $6 billion through extended-timeline financing. Wealthy asset holders capture $5 billion through the financialization premium. And barrier-protected developers capture about $3 billion in above-competitive returns. All four groups benefit from specific legal instruments. None of it is market reward for productive activity.

Slide 7 — Two Problems ~0:30

And this brings me to what I think is the most important finding — the one that reframes the whole policy debate.

Manhattan's housing crisis has two distinct problems that require two different solutions, and they are constantly conflated. Problem one is artificial extraction — the $613 per month above fair rent that exists because of legal distortions. This is solvable through regulatory reform. This is the problem market advocates are right about.

Problem two is that even the fair rent of $3,887 is unaffordable to 80% of NYC households. Manhattan's genuine production costs already exclude most of the city's residents, before any legal distortion enters the picture. This problem requires direct public investment in permanently affordable housing. This is what housing justice advocates are right about.

Both sides of this debate are talking about real problems. They're just talking about different parts of the same crisis — and acting as though fixing one fixes both.

Slide 8 — Conclusion ~0:15

To conclude. Manhattan's legally constructed housing premiums generate an estimated $22 billion annually in transfers from renters and buyers to landowners and financial institutions. This is not a market outcome. It is legally constructed extraction, traceable to the 1961 Zoning Resolution, ULURP procedures, and federal financial instruments.

The competitive market price is $770 per square foot. Fair rent for a one-bedroom is $3,887 per month. And even at that fair price, 80% of New York City households are priced out of Manhattan — not by a broken market, but by the genuine cost of building on one of the world's most productive pieces of land.

The policy implication: we need both regulatory reform and public investment — not one or the other. Thank you.
